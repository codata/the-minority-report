### Definition
Mevsimsel influenza, Dünya Sağlık Örgütü (WHO)’nın verilerine göre, dünya çapında yılda yaklaşık bir milyar vakaya, 3‑5 milyon şiddetli vakaya ve 290.000‑650.000 solunum yolu ölüme neden olur.  

### Primary reference(s)

### Annotations
#### Additional scientific description  
Mevsimsel influenza epidemikeleri, kuzey yarımkürenin Ekim‑Mart ve güney yarımkürenin Nisan‑Eylül dönemlerinde yoğunlaşır. Tropikal ve subtropikal ülkelerde ise yıl boyunca görülebilir. İnfluenza virüslerinin sürekli evrimleşen doğası, onları yüksek etki gücüne sahip bulaşıcı tehlikeler arasında tutar. Mevsimsel influenza genellikle tip A veya B influenza virüsleri tarafından tetiklenir. Yayılma, virüslerin yüzeyinde küçük değişikliklerle başlar ve halkın belirli bir bağışıklığa sahip olduğu virüslere karşı duyarlılığını azaltır.  

#### Symptoms  
- Sudden onset of fever  
- Dry cough  
- Headache  
- Myalgia and arthralgia  
- Sore throat  
- Rhinorrhoea  

Cough can be severe and persist for two or more weeks. Most individuals recover within a week without medical attention.  

#### High‑risk groups  
- Pregnant women  
- Children under five years  
- Individuals older than 65 years  
- Those with chronic medical conditions (cardiac, pulmonary, renal, metabolic, neurodevelopmental, hepatic, or hematologic diseases)  
- Immunosuppressive conditions/treatments (HIV, chemotherapy, steroids, malignancy)  

#### Management  
Non‑high‑risk patients: symptomatic treatment and home isolation to prevent community spread.  
High‑risk patients: antiviral therapy plus symptomatic treatment as soon as possible. Severe or progressive illness (pneumonia, sepsis, or exacerbation of chronic disease) warrants prompt antiviral therapy.  

#### Diagnosis  
Most human influenza cases are clinically diagnosed. Confirmation requires respiratory samples and laboratory tests:  
- Direct antigen detection  
- Virus isolation  
- Reverse transcriptase‑polymerase chain reaction (RT‑PCR)  

Samples: throat, nasal, and nasopharyngeal secretions; tracheal aspirate or washings.  

#### Vaccination  
WHO recommends annual vaccination with updated formulations for high‑risk groups, ideally before the season starts. Vaccination at any time during the season still reduces infection risk.  

### Metrics and numeric limits  
Worldwide, annual epidemics yield 3‑5 million severe cases and 290 000‑650 000 respiratory deaths. In industrialized nations, most influenza deaths occur in individuals 65 years or older. Clinics and hospitals may become overwhelmed during peak periods, leading to high absenteeism and productivity losses. In developing countries, 99 % of deaths in children under five with influenza‑associated lower respiratory tract infections occur.  

### Key relevant UN convention / multilateral treaty  
International Health Regulations (2005), 3rd ed. (WHO, 2016).  

### Drivers  
All age groups can be affected; however, pregnant women, children under five, older adults, and individuals with chronic or immunosuppressive conditions are at greater risk of severe disease or complications. Health‑care workers face high infection risk and may transmit the virus to vulnerable patients. Vaccination protects both workers and their contacts.  

### Impacts  
- Approximately one billion annual cases; 3‑5 million severe cases; 290 000‑650 000 respiratory deaths.  
- 95 % of deaths in children under five with influenza‑related lower respiratory tract infections occur in developing countries.  
- Severe cases can lead to pneumonia and sepsis. Hospitalization and death mainly affect high‑risk groups.  

### Multi‑hazard context  
Epidemics cause significant absenteeism and productivity losses. Seasonal influenza spreads rapidly in crowded settings such as schools and nursing homes. Transmission is facilitated by respiratory droplets and contaminated hands.  

### Risk Management  
- Cover mouth and nose with a tissue when coughing or sneezing; wash hands frequently.  
- Vaccination is the most effective prevention method.  
- Live attenuated nasal spray and inactivated injectable vaccines are available; annual vaccination is required due to continuous viral mutation.  

### Monitoring  
Global influenza surveillance relies on the WHO Global Influenza Surveillance and Response System (GISRS) established in 1952. GISRS facilitates worldwide confidence through virus sharing, data exchange, and collaborative research. FluNet is the web‑based tool for virological surveillance; it tracks subtype detection and informs epidemiological analyses.
