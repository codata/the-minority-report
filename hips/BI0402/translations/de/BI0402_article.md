### Definition
Widespread and heavy infestations of crops and natural vegetation by locusts that pose significant threats to food security, livelihoods, and natural habitats in multiple regions (adapted from FAO, 2009a).

### Primary reference(s)
FAO, 2009a. Glossary on Desert Locust. Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO). Zugriff am 27. Mai 2025.

### Annotations
**Additional scientific description**  
Locusts are members of a large group of insects commonly called grasshoppers, which possess large hind legs for jumping. They belong to the family *Acrididae* (FAO, 2025a). All grasshoppers belong to the superfamily *Acridoidea*, and the most significant species are all within the family *Acrididae*. Locusts differ from other grasshoppers in that they can alter their behaviour, physiology, colour, and morphology in response to changes in their population density. The life cycle of all locust and grasshopper species comprises three stages: egg, hopper, and adult (FAO, 2009a).  
A *Plage* of locusts is defined as a period of one or more years of widespread and heavy infestations, most of which occur as bands or swarms. A plague can occur when favourable breeding conditions are present and control operations fail to prevent a series of local outbreaks from developing into an unsustainable surge. A major plague exists when two or more regions are affected simultaneously (FAO, 2009a).  
The *Wüstenschrecke* (*Schistocerca gregaria*) can fly long distances and migrate very quickly. It is a transboundary pest whose control requires international cooperation. The same applies to about a dozen other locust species, which can produce outbreaks on every continent except Antarctica (FAO, 2015). During outbreaks, surges and plagues, locusts attack and destroy pastures and a wide range of cultivated crops, including cereals, cucurbits, legumes, sunflowers, tobacco, vegetables, vines, fruit trees, cotton, and other plants.  
The *Wüstenschrecke* is considered the most dangerous migratory pest in the world, threatening crop production and food security. It may be the oldest recorded pest for its danger and its ability to live and breed under diverse ecological and climatic regimes, across vast areas covering 29 million km² from the Atlantic Ocean in the west to India and Pakistan in the east (FAO, 2009a). During plagues, swarms can invade more than 60 of the world’s poorest countries and potentially damage the livelihoods of one‑tenth of the global population, mainly in Africa, the Middle East, and Asia (FAO, 2015).  
The worst *Wüstenschrecken*-outbreak of the last 25 years occurred in the Near East, the Greater Horn of Africa, and Southwest Asia in 2020‑2021 (FAO, 2022a). Tens of thousands of hectares of cropland and pasture were damaged in Ethiopia, Kenya, and Somalia where food security is already fragile. Consequently, 36.6 million people in affected countries faced crisis‑level food insecurity (FAO, 2022b).  
Other species besides *Wüstenschrecken* can also cause significant damage under favourable conditions in various parts of the world. These include the Italian locust, the Moroccan locust in the Caucasus and Central Asia, the Red locust in Eastern Africa, the Brown locust in Southern Africa, the Migratory locusts in Africa and Asia, the Tree locust in Africa, and the Australian Plague Locust in Australia (FAO, 2020c; 2020d).  
Locusts also pose serious threats to agriculture in the Caucasus and Central Asia. Country borders in the region often cross traditional locust habitats and breeding areas, and locusts frequently cross national political borders. Therefore, international cooperation is essential for their control in the region. Three main locust pests—the Asian Migratory Locust (*Locusta migratoria migratoria*), the Italian locust (*Calliptamus italicus*), and the Moroccan locust (*Dociostaurus maroccanus*)—threaten food security and livelihoods in both regions and in adjacent areas of northern Afghanistan and the southern Russian Federation, putting more than 25 million hectares of cultivated land at potential risk.

### Drivers
Locusts are known as the most destructive invasive plant pests. They have a high capacity to multiply, form groups, migrate over relatively large distances (up to 100 km per day), and settle and breed in various habitats. These capacities enhance their prevalence at the regional level. Highly mobile and capable of stripping an area’s vegetation, locust swarms can cause large‑scale agricultural and environmental damage. Even a relatively small swarm can consume the same amount of food in one day as about 35 000 people. This can be especially devastating in countries facing food‑security crises, where every gram of produced food helps alleviate hunger (FAO, 2020c).

### Impacts
Locusts can change their behaviour and appearance under particular environmental conditions (e.g., unusually heavy rains) and transform from harmless individuals into a collective mass of insects that forms swarms. During quiet periods (known as *Rückgangsphasen*), solitarious locusts are found in low numbers scattered throughout the deserts of North Africa, the Middle East, and Southwest Asia (*Wüstenschrecke*). This arid area covers about 16 million km² and includes roughly 30 countries. It is called the *Rückgangszone*. In severe cases, swarms can invade an area equivalent to about 20 % of the Earth’s surface (FAO, 2015).  
Locust surges may cause food‑security challenges, especially if staple crops are affected. They can also lead to market or price instability. In certain cases, such infestations can cause severe food insecurity, as seen during *Wüstenschrecken*-surges in the Horn of Africa and Yemen in 2020‑2021 (FAO, 2022b). In addition to impacts on food security, locusts can devastate natural landscapes. Other implications for local populations include local conflicts or migration due to food shortages in severe surges. Moreover, in some cases, insecticide sprays may affect the health of local inhabitants.

### Risk Management
Improving national capacities in preparedness, contingency planning, surveillance, and rapid response is key for preventing such infestations and mitigating impacts. Likewise, international data and knowledge sharing is essential to address transboundary infestations and surges. A good example of international collaboration is the expansion of fall armyworms across Africa and Asia. The FAO has developed and runs a global initiative (FAO, 2020c).  
Concerning the benefits of international collaboration and a robust emergency response, lessons can be learned from the locust surges in 2020‑2021 in the Horn of Africa and Yemen. The FAO‑led response effort averted 4.5 million tonnes of crop losses, saved 900 million litres of milk production, and secured food for 41.5 million people (FAO, 2022a).  
The main strategy for locust management, promoted by the FAO for decades, relies on the so‑called preventive locust control strategy. This is based on appropriate monitoring of locust habitats at key development periods to enable early detection of population increases and behavioural changes, early warning, and early reaction (FAO, 2020d). When well implemented, this approach reduces the occurrence and intensity of locust outbreaks and prevents their development into major surges or plagues. Over decades, the strategy has proven to be the most effective, enabling a response before a significant population increase can occur. It results in reduced damage to crops and pastures, thereby increasing food security and improving livelihoods of highly vulnerable rural communities; reduced negative impacts on human health and the environment (through fewer pesticide sprays); and lower financial costs. For such transboundary plant pests, this strategy must also be coupled with global or regional cooperation.  
FAO’s recommendations for dealing with locust surges in the Greater Horn of Africa Region include:  

1. **Ground surveillance and impact assessments** – Facilitate ground surveillance, monitoring, and continuous assessment in partnership with national governments and the Desert Locust Control Organization for Eastern Africa (DLCO‑EA) to strengthen region‑wide early warning and response.  
2. **Informing response** – Continue assessing the situation through the FAO Desert Locust Information Service and provide early warning, forecasts, and advice to affected countries and international partners.  
3. **Control operations** – Provide aerial and ground control operations support and enhance national preparedness capacity.  
4. **Ground control operations (hopper‑stage locusts)** – Procure chemical and biopesticides and equipment, store them, train personnel, ensure human and environmental safety, and dispose of chemical drums and containers.  
5. **Air control operations (adult‑stage locusts)** – Contract planes, procure pesticides, ensure safety, train personnel, and dispose of drums and containers.  

On average, over the past 15 years, locust‑affected areas as large as almost 4 million hectares have been treated annually in the Caucasus and Central Asia.

**Which institution(s) produce(s) Disaster Risk Data/Information?**  
The most efficient monitoring and early warning system is the desert locust monitoring system operated by the FAO.  
- Desert Locust Information Service (DLIS) and the three commissions (Western Region, Central Region, and Southwest Asia) as well as the Desert Locust Control Committee (DLCC) (FAO, 2025a).

**How is the hazard observed/monitored/forecast?**  
The system utilizes a network of concerned countries that conduct surveys and share data via satellite tools with the Desert Locust Information Service (DLIS), which has been operating since 1978 (FAO, 2025b) and is headquartered at the FAO, where the data is analysed together with geospatial and meteorological data. Analysis helps monitor the overall situation and produce forecasts and early warnings for potential outbreaks to be shared publicly with the concerned countries.
