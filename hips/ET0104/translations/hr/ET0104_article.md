### Definition
Solar energetic particle (SEP) events occur when a large‑scale magnetic eruption, often accompanied by a coronal mass ejection (CME) and/or a related solar flare, accelerates charged particles in the solar atmosphere to significant fractions of the speed of light. The primary particles of interest are protons. SEP arrive with diverse fluxes and energies at different geographic locations (NOAA, 2023).

### Annotations  
#### Additional scientific description  
Upon reaching Earth, the high‑speed protons penetrate through the magnetosphere, which acts as a protective shield against lower‑energy charged particles. Once inside the magnetosphere, the particles follow the guidance of magnetic field lines and primarily enter the Earth’s atmosphere near the north and south poles. However, the more energetic particles can also reach the low‑latitude upper atmosphere. SEP events can severely affect space hardware, disrupt radio communications, and cause conditions for re‑routing commercial air traffic away from polar regions. Neutron monitors can be used to record the particle flux intensity at different regions of the planet.

### Metrics and numeric limits  
Solar energetic particle events are classified using a five‑level scale introduced by the United States National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) in 1999 (NOAA, 2023). The scale is currently under review and presented below.

| Scale | Description | Physical measure (Flux ≥ 10 MeV particles /s·ster·cm²) | Average Frequency (1 cycle = 11 years) |
|-------|-------------|--------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **S5** | Extreme | Biological: unavoidable high radiation hazard to astronauts on EVA; passengers and crew in high‑flying aircraft at high latitudes may be exposed to radiation risk. <br> Satellite operations: satellites may be rendered useless; memory impacts can cause loss of control; serious noise in image data; star‑trackers may be unable to locate sources; permanent damage to solar panels possible. <br> Other systems: complete blackout of HF communications possible through the polar regions; navigation position errors make operations extremely difficult. | 0.105 / cycle |
| **S4** | Severe | Biological: unavoidable radiation hazard to astronauts on EVA; passengers and crew in high‑flying aircraft at high latitudes may be exposed to radiation risk. <br> Satellite operations: may experience memory device problems and noise on imaging systems; star‑tracker problems may cause orientation issues; solar panel efficiency can be degraded. <br> Other systems: blackout of HF radio communications through the polar regions and increased navigation errors over several days are likely. | 1 / cycle |
| **S3** | Strong | Biological: radiation hazard avoidance recommended for astronauts on EVA; passengers and crew in high‑flying aircraft at high latitudes may be exposed to radiation risk. <br> Satellite operations: single‑event upsets, noise in imaging systems, slight reduction of efficiency in solar panels are likely. <br> Other systems: degraded HF radio propagation through the polar regions; navigation position errors likely. | 3 / cycle |
| **S2** | Moderate | Biological: passengers and crew in high‑flying aircraft at high latitudes may be exposed to elevated radiation risk. <br> Satellite operations: infrequent single‑event upsets possible. <br> Other systems: small effects on HF propagation through the polar regions; navigation at polar cap locations possibly affected. | 5 / cycle |
| **S1** | Minor | Biological: none. <br> Satellite operations: none. <br> Other systems: minor impacts on HF radio in the polar regions. | 10 / cycle |

The Space Weather centres designated by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) are expected to provide airliners with advisories about enhanced radiation levels at flight altitudes when the dose rates due to a solar proton event exceed 30 µSv h⁻¹ (MOD) and 80 µSv h⁻¹ (SEV) (ICAO, 2019).

Not applicable. However, radiation exposure limits are indirectly governed by ICAO and ICRP guidelines.

### Drivers  
Solar eruptive events: Solar energetic particle events occur during large‑scale magnetic eruptions on the Sun, often associated with coronal mass ejections (CMEs) and solar flares. These events result from the acceleration of charged particles, primarily protons, to high velocities.

### Impacts  
- **Particle exposure** – Solar energetic particles, especially high‑energy protons, pose hazards to astronauts in space and airline crew members during high‑altitude flights. Elevated fluxes during SEP events can exceed safety limits, affecting software and hardware in aircraft avionics.  
- **Satellite anomalies and failures** – Solar energetic particles can cause disruptions and damage to satellites. High‑energy particles colliding with sensitive electronic components, especially solar panels, deteriorate or prevent communication and navigation capabilities.  
- **Radiation exposure of flight crews and passengers** – SEPs increase radiation exposure, potentially requiring flight rerouting to lower latitudes. Trans‑polar routes are most affected.  
- **Health risks for astronauts** – Increased particle radiation doses pose serious health risks and affect mission planning and execution. Long‑term risks exist for populations exposed to cumulative flux during SEP events.

### Risk Management  
- **Space weather monitoring** – Space weather services monitor solar activity and provide alerts and forecasts for potential SEP events, helping detect and assess risks.  
- **Particle flux forecasting** – Advanced models and data analysis techniques forecast the flux and intensity of solar energetic particles during SEP events, enabling operators to evaluate risks and take precautions.  
- **Radiation shielding** – Astronauts and airline crew members receive radiation shielding (lead or polyethylene). Spacecraft and satellites are equipped with shielding to protect sensitive components.  
- **Flight re‑routing and altitude restrictions** – During heightened solar activity, airlines may consider re‑routing flights away from high‑polar regions or adjusting altitudes to reduce exposure. ICAO’s SWX advisory system helps airlines mitigate exposure.

### Monitoring  
Space weather services from the International Space Environment Services (ISES) provide warning systems for specific users in their countries. The Space Weather centres designated by ICAO give airliners advisories about SEPs.
