### Definition  
Prionas slimības ir reti, progresīvi neirodegeneratīvi traucējumi, kas ietekmē gan cilvēkus, gan dzīvniekus (pielāgots no CDC, 2024, un WHO, datuma nav).

### Primary reference(s)  
CDC, 2024. Prion Diseases. Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).  
WHO, no date. Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathies.

### Annotations  

#### Additional scientific description  
Šie traucējumi ietekmē zveru nervu sistēmu. Lai gan precīzs pārraides mehānisms vēl nav zināms, pieņemams, ka tas ir saistīts ar infekciju proteīnu, sauktu prions, un ar prionu proteīnu PrP vai PrPc, kas pārvēršas par anomālu priona proteīnu, saukto PrPsc (WHO, datuma nav). Traucējumi rodas, kad ķermenī normāli esošie proteīni kļūst nepareizi savienoti, izraisot slimību. Proteīnu struktūras pārveidošana noved pie smadzeņu bojājumiem un citiem simptomiem. Simptomi var ilgt gadu laiku, un vienreiz sākoties slimība strauji progresē un noved pie nāves. Viena no iemesliem, kāpēc cilvēki var attīstīt priona slimību, ir pārtikas vai dzīvnieku mīklas iejutēšana vai apstrāde ar piesārņotām prioniem. Cilvēki var arī kļūt infekti, ja prioni ieiet viņu ķermenī. Tas var notikt caur orgānu transplantāciju vai iejaugu, kas piesārņotas ar prioniem (CDC, 2024).

Termins “pārraidāmās spongfemā encefalopijas” (TSE) ietver:  

- Creutzfeldta‑Jakoba slimība (CJD) cilvēkos  
- Scrapie ziežļu lopās  
- Goat‑chronic wasting disease (goats)  
- Bovine spongfemā encefalopija (BSE) piena lopām (WHO, datuma nav)

#### Human  

Daudzas priona slimības ietekmē cilvēkus, bet dažas ir biežākas nekā citas. Visbiežāk izplatītā priona slimība cilvēkos ir Creutzfeldta‑Jakoba slimība (CJD). CJD ir priona slimība, kas izraisa smadzeņu bojājumus. Kad simptomi sākas, slimība strauji progresē. Tā vienmēr ir fatāla, parasti vienu gadu laikā. CJD tiek dažkārt saukta par “klasisko CJD”, lai izvairītos no pārpratumiem ar variantu CJD. Lielākā daļa klasiskās CJD gadījumu ir vecākiem cilvēkiem. Tie parādās sporadiāli, tas nozīmē, ka iemesliem, kāpēc gadījumi sākas, nav zināmas. Mazākie gadījumi ir ģenētiski, kas padara cilvēkus jutīgus CJD. Reizēm ir bijušas arī pacientiem, kas tika izsistami ar ķirurģiskām iekārtām vai citiem izsistamiem produktiem, piesārņoti ar prioniem (CDC, 2024).

Cita priona slimība, variantu Creutzfeldta‑Jakoba slimība (vCJD), ir ar līdzīgu nosaukumu, bet atšķirīga un daudz retāk izstārstīta (CDC, 2024). vCJD ir reti sastopams, fatāls cilvēku neirodegeneratīvs stāvoklis, ko pirmo reizi aprakstīja 1996. gada martā. Tāpat kā CJD, vCJD tiek klasificēta kā TSE, ņemot vērā tipisku spongfemā degenerāciju smadzeņos un spējību tikt pārraidīta. vCJD attīstība ir cieši saistīta ar piesārņojumu, iespējams, caur mīklu, kas ieguvusi prionus no BSE, ko pirmo reizi ziņoja Lielbritānijā 1986. gadā. Pretējā gadījumā, salīdzinot ar tradicionālajiem CJD veidiem (sporādi, ģenētiski un iatroģēni), vCJD ietekmēja jaunos pacientus vidējā vecumā 29 gadi, salīdzinot ar >65 gadiem citiem veidiem. vCJD ir relatīvi ilgs slimības ilgums, vidēji 14 mēneši, salīdzinot ar 4,5 mēnešiem CJD (WHO, datuma nav).

Creutzfeldta‑Jakoba slimība (vCJD) ir saistīta ar BSE, ko tika atklāta 1996. gadā Apvienotajā Karalistē. Slimība ietekmēja daudz jaunākus cilvēkus nekā CJD. vCJD bieži infekcijas cilvēkus 20. gadu vai jaunākos. Tā izraisīja prāta simptomus, uzvedības izmaiņas, kā arī dysesthesijas, ko CJD nerada (CDC, 2024).

Citi priona traucējumi ietver kuru, variablu protease-atsaucamības priona patoloģiju, sporādizējošas, fatālie slimības, un ģenētiski nosacītās formas, kas izraisītas ar ģenētiskām mutācijām (CDC, 2024). Tas ir neiekaisīgs neirodegeneratīvs saslimums un ir viena no pārraidāmā spongfemā encefalopijas formām, kas tiek pārraidīta caur kanibālismu (Asher, 2018). Kuru simptomu progresija ir ātra, ar cerebellārām un ekstrapiramīdālām zīmēm un simptomiem; nāve notiek vienu līdz diviem gadiem no simptomu sākuma (Gajdusek, 1957).

Pasaules Veselības organizācija (WHO) publicēja vadlīnijas par gadījumu klasifikāciju un uzraudzības standartu vCJD un citām cilvēku pārraidāmām priona slimībām (WHO, 2003).

### Drivers  
Acquired human prion diseases are rare. In these instances, the most common sources are medical procedures, where an individual may be exposed to abnormal prion protein via contaminated equipment or certain transplanted human tissues, or via ingestion of meat from cattle infected with BSE (linked to vCJD). Other prion diseases such as scrapie and chronic wasting disease can be transmitted between animals; however, transmission from animals to humans has only been observed in BSE/vCJD (WHO, 2003). There is also concern about the possibility that bovine‑derived materials involved in the production of vaccines and other pharmaceutical products could represent a potential route of transmission. Foetal calf serum is a widespread component of growth media, and other media components such as enzymes and hormones are extracted from cattle. Additionally, bovine materials have been used in various products such as gelatin, a component of capsules. An intense regulatory effort has therefore been made to eliminate materials potentially contaminated with BSE from all stages of production. Where bovine materials are still required, they must be sourced from geographical areas that are not endemic for BSE. In addition, difficult regulatory decisions have been necessary where the relative risks inherent in replacing fully characterized materials (such as vaccine seed strains) are compared with the mathematically small risk of vCJD transmission. The field is also complicated by the difficulty in assaying materials for TSEs and in conducting studies to detect major routes of transmission and infection of this highly novel class of pathogen (WHO, no date). In 2006, a WHO report stated that the eventual impact of human exposures to the BSE agent and secondary transmissions of vCJD via blood, blood components, and plasma‑derived products cannot be predicted (WHO, 2006). It also reported that blood programs and national authorities should consider carefully, in advance, the potential impact of screening donors for TSE, including the implications of notifying persons testing positive, their families, and society, as well as on the blood supply (WHO, 2006).

### Impacts  
Bovine spongfemā encefalopija (BSE) is a progressive, fatal disease of the nervous system of bovines that is caused by the accumulation of an abnormal protein called ‘prion’ in nervous tissue. Two forms can be distinguished: classical BSE (caused by C‑type BSE agent), which occurs in bovines after ingesting prion‑contaminated feed, and atypical BSE (caused by H‑ and L‑type agents), which is believed to occur spontaneously in all bovine populations. Classical BSE was first detected in 1986, and the implementation of appropriate control measures resulted in its decline worldwide. To date, the incidence of both forms is negligible and estimated to approach zero cases per million bovines. Classical BSE is considered zoonotic due to its assumed link with the emergence of variant Creutzfeldt‑Jakob disease in humans. Classical BSE is a WOAH‑listed disease, for which WOAH has established official recognition of sanitary risk status (WOAH, 2023).

### Multi‑hazard context  
Concern about iatrogenic (i.e., illness caused by medical examination or treatment) spread of prion‑related disease from the use of contaminated corneal and dura mater grafts, neurosurgical equipment, and cadaver‑derived human growth hormone, as well as blood‑borne transmission of vCJD, has long been suspected as possible because of some unusual features of the disease (Belay & Schonberger, 2005).

### Risk Management  
In order to update the preventive measures proposed by the World Health Organization (WHO) in 1997 to minimize the risks associated with the use of vaccines, blood products and other pharmaceutical products containing bovine‑derived and human‑derived materials, a meeting of international experts was convened at WHO in Geneva on 3‑5 February 2003. The purpose was to review the latest available data on the epidemiology, the antemortem and post‑mortem diagnosis, detection of the infectious agents, and distribution of infectivity in tissues or body fluids of relevant species with TSEs (WHO, 2003).

### Monitoring  
WHO supports countries to conduct all‑hazards strategic risk assessment in the contexts of health emergencies and disasters, which results in the development of a country risk profile. Empowered with the country risk profile, inclusive of a seasonal risk calendar, countries can anticipate potential emergencies before they occur to trigger early alerts and inform early actions (WHO, 2021). WHO's Early Warning, Alert and Response System (EWARS) has been designed to improve disease outbreak detection in emergency settings, such as in conflict‑affected countries or following a natural disaster. It is a simple and cost‑effective way to rapidly set up a disease surveillance system. EWARS is deployed during an emergency as an adjunct to the national disease surveillance system. WHO works with Ministries of Health and health sector partners to train local health workers to use the system. After the emergency, EWARS should reintegrate into the national system (WHO, no date).
