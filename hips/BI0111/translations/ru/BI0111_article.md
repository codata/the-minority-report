# Definition

Криптоспориум (*Cryptosporidium*) — микроскопический паразит, способный жить в воде, еде, почве и на поверхностях, загрязненных инфицированными фекалиями, и вызывать водянистую диарею, известную как криптоспориоз (адаптировано из CDC 2024 и Peletz et al., 2013).

# Annotations

## Additional scientific description

**Human cryptosporidiosis** is a diarrhoea‑causing disease that spreads through germs residing in the faeces of infected humans or animals. When a person ingests *Cryptosporidium* (or “Crypto” germs), infection can occur. Symptoms generally begin 2–10 days (average 7 days) after infection and last 1–2 weeks. The most common symptom is watery diarrhoea, but some people are asymptomatic (CDC, 2024a).  
Cryptosporidiosis is a disease that causes watery diarrhoea, caused by microscopic parasites called *Cryptosporidium*. Both the parasite and the disease are commonly known as “Crypto” (CDC, 2024).

Although cryptosporidiosis can affect all people, some groups are likely to develop more serious illness. For individuals with weakened immune systems, symptoms can be severe and may lead to life‑threatening disease. Cryptosporidiosis is endemic worldwide; the highest rates are found in low‑ and middle‑income countries. An estimated 823 000 cases occur in the United States each year, of which 9.9 % are thought to be due to international travel. The highest US rates are reported among young children aged 1–4 years and adults aged 15–44 years, particularly females (likely caregivers changing diapers and helping with toileting). International travel is a risk factor for sporadic cryptosporidiosis in the United States (population attributable risk is 11 %) and other high‑income nations; few studies have assessed prevalence among travellers (CDC, 2024b).

Symptoms generally begin two to ten days (average seven days) after infection and include watery diarrhoea (most common), abdominal cramps or pain, dehydration, nausea, vomiting, fever and weight loss. Some people can shed *Cryptosporidium* despite being asymptomatic (CDC, 2024).  
Symptoms usually last about one to two weeks (range a few days to a month or more) in persons with healthy immune systems. Occasionally, people may experience recurrence after a brief recovery. Symptoms can recur for up to 30 days.

Individuals with weakened immune systems (e.g., HIV, cancer or transplant patients on immunosuppressive drugs, inherited immune‑deficiency disorders) may develop chronic illness or life‑threatening complications affecting digestion or absorption, and may experience weight loss and wasting. Recent evidence from high‑HIV‑prevalence regions shows cryptosporidiosis to be more common and associated with higher diarrhoeal morbidity and mortality in young children than previously believed. The disease has been linked to relatively high case fatality and nutritional stunting (Peletz et al., 2013).

**Animal cryptosporidiosis** – *Cryptosporidium* can infect a wide range of vertebrate hosts, including birds, reptiles, and mammals (CDC, 2024c). *C. parvum* primarily infects the gastrointestinal tract and is a major cause of diarrhoea in young, unweaned livestock. Mortality is generally low, but severe outbreaks may occur occasionally. Weaned and adult animals usually do not show clinical signs but can excrete oocysts that contaminate the environment, facilitating onward transmission. No commercial vaccine is available for cryptosporidiosis (WOAH, 2022).

## Metrics and numeric limits

In 2016, *Cryptosporidium* infection was the fifth leading diarrhoeal aetiology in children under 5 years, causing more than 48 000 deaths and more than 4.2 million disability‑adjusted life‑years lost (Khalil et al., 2018).

## Drivers

Strong evidence links transmission of *Cryptosporidium* to inadequate sanitation, unsafe drinking water, and poor hygiene. *Cryptosporidium* is a major zoonotic, waterborne pathogen with many documented drinking‑water outbreaks (WHO, 2025). Transmission is primarily faecal‑oral. *C. hominis* and *C. parvum* are the main human‑pathogenic species. Livestock, particularly young cattle and sheep in the pre‑weaning period, are important sources of zoonotic *C. parvum* (WHO, 2025). *Cryptosporidium* infection is worldwide; the resulting illness is characterised by prolonged, watery diarrhoea that is usually self‑limiting. Malnourished young children and severely immunocompromised individuals may suffer chronic, severe diarrhoea with significant fluid loss and increased morbidity and mortality. *Cryptosporidium* is one of the major causes of morbidity and mortality from diarrhoeal disease in malnourished young children (WHO, 2025).  
It is a small protozoan parasite that infects the microvillous region of epithelial cells in the digestive and respiratory tracts of vertebrates. It is an obligate intracellular parasite of humans and other mammals, birds, reptiles, and fish. It requires its host to multiply. Environmentally robust oocysts are shed by infected hosts into the environment and can survive adverse conditions for months until ingested by a new host. In the new host, the life cycle restarts, and multiplication occurs using the host’s resources (WHO, 2009).

## Impacts

Reported incidence of *Cryptosporidium* ranges from <1 % in high‑income countries to 8 % in low‑ and middle‑income countries. In 2016, *Cryptosporidium* infection was the fifth leading diarrhoeal aetiology in children under 5 years, causing >48 000 deaths and >4.2 million disability‑adjusted life‑years lost (Khalil et al., 2018).

## Multi‑hazard context

Outbreaks of *Cryptosporidium* have been reported in hospitals, daycare centres, households, among people sharing the same food, during recreational water activities (lakes, swimming pools), and in municipalities with contaminated public water supplies. Water distribution systems are particularly vulnerable to contamination with *Cryptosporidium*, which can survive most disinfection procedures such as chlorination (ECDC, no date).

## Risk Management

*Cryptosporidium* lives in the intestines of infected humans or animals. An infected person or animal sheds *Cryptosporidium* parasites in stool. Millions of parasites can be released in a bowel movement. Shedding begins when symptoms start and can last weeks after symptoms (e.g., diarrhoea) disappear. People become infected after accidentally swallowing the parasite (CDC, 2024). *Cryptosporidium* is transmitted via the faecal‑oral route. Its low infectious dose, prolonged survival in moist environments, protracted communicability, and extreme chlorine tolerance make it ideally suited for transmission through contaminated drinking or recreational water (e.g., swimming pools). Transmission can also occur through contact with faecally contaminated surfaces, by eating contaminated food, or through contact with infected animals (particularly pre‑weaned bovine calves) or people (e.g., during direct care, during oral–anal sex). Routine testing for ova and parasites does not usually include *Cryptosporidium*; specifically request testing for this organism when infection is suspected. New molecular enteric panel assays generally include *Cryptosporidium* as a target pathogen (CDC 2024b). Other diagnostic techniques include microscopy with direct fluorescent antibody (considered the gold standard), enzyme immunoassay kits, molecular assays, microscopy with modified acid‑fast staining, and rapid immunochromatographic cartridge assays (CDC 2024b). Infections caused by different *Cryptosporidium* species and subtypes can differ clinically. Most species, all with multiple subtypes, are indistinguishable by traditional diagnostic tests (CDC, 2024b). Travellers can reduce their risk for cryptosporidiosis by carefully adhering to food and water precautions and using proper hand‑washing techniques. To protect themselves, swimmers should avoid ingesting recreational water. To protect others, infected people should not enter recreational water while ill with diarrhoea, and for the first 2 weeks after symptoms have completely resolved, because of prolonged excretion of infectious oocysts. Practising safer sex (i.e., reducing contact with faeces) can also decrease the risk of infection (CDC, 2024b).
