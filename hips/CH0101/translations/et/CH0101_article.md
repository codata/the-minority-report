### Definition  
Arsen on mürgine metaloid, mis on laialdaselt jaetud Maa kraasides, tavaliselt arsenisulfidina või metallarsedatite ja arselite kujul. Inimese ekspositsioon arseini ühenditele kujutab endast suurepärast avaliku tervise ohut. See on seostatud mitmete akutsete ja pikaajaliste negatiivsete tervise mõjudega ning haigustega (WHO, 2019).

### Additional scientific description  
Arsen (keemiline sümbol As, aatomarv 33) võib atmosfääri ja vees vabastada järgmistel viisidel:  
- looduslikud tegevused, näiteks vulkaanikus, mineraalide lahustumine või desorptioon (eriti sügavõrgu vees), taimete eksudaatid ja tuulese kaevu tolm;  
- inimtegevused, näiteks metallide sulatamine, fossiilkütuste põletamine (eriti süsinik), kaevandus, puitkaitse lahuste ja ajalooliselt põllumajanduspestitsiidide tootmine ja kasutamine;  
- ajalooliste allikate re-mobiliseerimine, näiteks kaevanduste drainagevesi; ja  
- teekonnatõmbudest sügavõrgu vee abil tarbimiskeskkonda arseini liikumine (WHO, 2019).  

Arseini ühendused on kasutatud keemilisena relvana (OPCW, 2024).  
Vees esineb arseini kahes peamiselt vormis: arsite (As(III)) redutseerivate tingimuste all ja arsentat (As(V)), kui vesi on hapnikustatud.  

Arsenit saab atmosfääri päästa peamiselt triioksiidina, eriti kõrgtemperatuursetel protsessidel või aerooneeritud mullal toimuvate volatilisatsiooniprotsesside kaudu. Atmosfääris on arsein peamiselt kaetud osakettidega, mida tuuled levitavad ja mis on kaduvad maapinnale ja veele (WHO, 2019).  

Anorgaaniline arsein on äärmiselt akutelt mürgistav. Pikaajaline anorgaanilise arseini tarbimine võib viia kroonilise arseini mürgistuse (arsenikosis) juurde. Mõjutused, mis võivad aastaid kuluda, sõltuvad ekspositsiooni tasemest, hõlmavad naha lüsioone, perifeerse neerutiit, gastrointestinalseid sümptomite, diabeedi, südame-veresoonkonna haiguste, arengukriitikat ja naha- ning siseorganite vähke (IARC, 2018). Orgaanilised arseini ühendid, mis on suures koguses mere- ja kala toidus, on vähem kahjulikud tervisele ja organismi poolt kiiresti välistatud.  

Inimene saab arseiniga ja arseini ühenditega eksposeeritud nii keskkondlikult kui ka töökoha kaudu. Kõrgete anorgaanilise arseini tasemetega ekspositsioon toimub peamiselt sügavõrgu vee kaudu, mis sisaldab loomulikult kõrgeid arseini tasemeid, toidukultuuride valmistamisel selle vee kasutamisega ja toidutootjate kastmisega kõrge arseini sisaldusega veega. Avaliku tervise toiminguid tuleb jätkata, et vähendada inimeste ekspositsiooni arseiniga, eriti piirkondades, kus sügavõrgu vesi sisaldab loomulikult kõrgeid tasemeid (WHO, 2019).  

### Metrics and numeric limits  
2002. aastal teatati Maailma Tervise Organisatsioonilt (WHO), et vähemalt 140 miljonit inimest 50 riigis oli tarbivusvett, mis sisaldas arseini taset, mis ületas WHO provisorilise juhise 10 µg/L (WHO, 2019). Anorgaaniline arsein esineb loomulikult kõrgest tasemest sügavõrgus mitmes riigis, sealhulgas Argentiinas, Chile, Hiinas, Indias (Lääne-Bengal), Meksikas, Ameerika Ühendriikides ja eriti Bangladeshis, kus 2012. aastal hinnati, et ligikaudu 19 miljonit inimest oli tarbivusvett, mille arseini konsentratsioon ületas 50 µg/L, ja 39 miljonit inimest tarbivad vesi, mille arseini tasemega ületas 10 µg/L. 2010. aastal oli Bangladeshi kõrge mõjutatud piirkonnas 21,4 % kõikidest surmatest põhjustatud arseini tasemetest, mis ületasid 10 µg/L tarbivusvees, samas kui 2012. aasta teises analüüsis kogu piirkondadel näidati umbes 43 000 surma aastas (umbes 5,6 % kõigist surmadest) ja nende põhjustaks krooniline arseinägemine (WHO, 2019).  

WHO on avaldanud arseini juhised kannatustasetest, tarbivusveest ja õhus (WHO, 2019):  
- **Kõlblik igapäevane tarbimine:** 0,5 % suurenenud kopsuõhuvähi sündimussageduse jaoks (BMDL0,5) 3,0 µg/kg kehakaalu päevas (2–7 µg/kg kehakaalu päevas).  
- **Tarbi­v­usvett:** Provisoriline juhis on 10 µg/L. Arsein eemaldamiseks tasub teha kõik jõupingutused, et hoida konsentratsioonid võimalikult madalad ja all hoida juhise.  
- **Õhk:** Ohutu arseini tase õhus ei saa kindlaks määrata.

### Drivers  
Tarbi­v­usvett kujutab avaliku tervise suurimat ohut arseini suhtes (WHO, 2019). Suitsetajate arseinesteks ekspositsiooniks on põhjustatud nende loodusliku anorgaanilise arseini sisaldusega sigarettid. Suitsetamisest varasemate aegade kõrgemad tasemed tulid sellest, et toidulisandites oli kasutatud arseinit sisaldavat looduslikku arseini (WHO, 2019).  

### Impacts  
Industrial processes uses the arsen to produce antifungal wood preservatives, which can lead to soil contamination. Other current or historical uses occur within the pharmaceutical and glass industries, in the manufacture of alloys, sheep dips, leather preservatives, arsenic-containing pigments, antifouling paints and poison baits and, to a diminishing extent, in the production of agrochemicals (especially for use in orchards and vineyards). Arsenic compounds are also employed in limited amounts in the microelectronics and optical industries. High arsenic levels in air can be found in the working environment as well as the general environment around non-ferrous metal smelters, where arsenic trioxide may be formed, and some coal-fired power plants (especially those using low-grade brown coal) (WHO, 2019). In areas where arsenic is not naturally present at high levels, food usually contributes most to the daily intake of arsenic. Fish, shellfish, meat, poultry, dairy products and cereals are the main sources of dietary intake. However, the arsenic in fish and shellfish is usually in the form of organic compounds (e.g., arsenobetaine) that are of low toxicity. In areas where arsenic is naturally present at high levels, food (e.g., rice) prepared with high arsenic-containing water and food crops irrigated with contaminated water also contribute to total daily intake (WHO, 2019). Accidents involving arsenic have occurred in various contexts, with significant impacts on human health and the environment. High concentrations of naturally occurring arsenic poisoning in drinking water have resulted in multiple poisoning episodes worldwide. An example from the 1950’s and 1960’s was the Taiwan Arsenic Poisoning (Blackfoot Disease), which led to the development of a condition known as Blackfoot Disease, characterized by peripheral vascular disease and gangrene. The contamination was traced to artesian well water with high levels of arsenic (Lai, 1994). A second example is the Bangladesh Arsenic Crisis in the 1990s, where a widespread contamination of arsenic from shallow tube wells led millions of exposed to high levels of arsenic in drinking water to various health issues, including skin lesions, cancers (such as skin, lung, and bladder cancer), cardiovascular diseases, and neurological disorders (Smith, 2000). Chemical spills have had a negative impact on the environment. An example is the Sandoz Chemical Spill which occurred in 1986. A fire at a Sandoz chemical warehouse in Basel, Switzerland, led to the release of various toxic chemicals, including arsenic compounds, into the Rhine River. The contamination caused widespread fish kills and had significant ecological impacts on the river ecosystem (Capel, 1988). Historical use of lead arsenate as a pesticide in agriculture led to soil contamination in many regions. Accidental ingestion or inhalation of soil particles containing lead and arsenic residues, particularly by farm workers and children living in treated areas, has resulted in cases of acute poisoning and long-term health effects (Lasky, 1985). The health of inhabitants of communities near to mining activities can be affected by arsenic presence. In a region of Colombia where gold mining activities are developed, genotoxicity and mutagenicity in blood and drinking water of exposed population were induced by arsenic. The results showed DNA damage related to arsenic in blood (p < 0.05) in the exposed population, with arsenic levels above the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) limit of 1 µg/L. Mutagenic activity was detected in the drinking water, with only one sample exceeding the WHO's maximum permissible arsenic concentration of 10 µg/L (Calao-Ramos, 2023).

### Risk Management  
Long-term actions are required to reduce exposure to arsen from mining, metal smelting and refining, combustion of low‑grade coal, pesticide use and timber treatment. In particular, action is needed to reduce the intake of arsen from drinking‑water and food in areas with naturally high levels in the groundwater (WHO, 2019). The WHO factsheet on preventing disease through healthy environments (WHO, 2019) includes the following risk mitigation recommendations:  

- Make available drinking‑water with arsen concentrations below the WHO provisional drinking‑water guideline value of 10 µg/L in areas where the level is higher.  
- Possible measures include:  
  - Testing water for arsen levels and informing users of the results.  
  - Installing arsen removal systems, either centralised or domestic, and ensuring appropriate disposal of the removed arsen.  
  - Substituting high‑arsenic sources, such as groundwater, with low‑arsenic, microbiologically safe sources such as rainwater and treated surface water. Low‑arsenic water can be used for drinking, cooking and irrigation purposes, whereas high‑arsenic water can be used for other purposes such as bathing and washing clothes.  
  - Discriminating between high‑arsenic and low‑arsenic sources by testing water for arsen levels and painting tube wells or hand pumps in different colours (e.g., red and green).  
  - Blending low‑arsenic water with higher‑arsenic water to achieve an acceptable arsen concentration level.  
  - Reduce occupational exposure to arsen and its compounds.  
  - Make both the general public and the health sector aware of the harmful effects of high‑arsenic intake and the sources of exposure (including use of high‑arsenic water for crop irrigation or food preparation) and how to avoid these sources.  
  - Monitor high‑risk populations for early signs of arsen poisoning, usually skin problems. It should be noted that total urinary arsen does not differentiate between inorganic arsen, which is toxic, and organic arsen, some of which is not. Where possible, arsen speciation should be attempted in order to differentiate these two forms (and their metabolites).  
- The WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene monitors progress towards global targets on drinking water. In addition, under the new 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, the indicator of ‘safely managed drinking water services’ calls for tracking the population accessing drinking water that is free of faecal contamination and priority chemical contaminants, including arsen (UN Water, no date; Council of Europe, 2021). The documented accidents in the impacts section underscore the importance of monitoring and regulating arsen exposure, implementing safe drinking water practices, and promoting alternative agricultural methods to minimize the risks associated with arsen contamination.

Actions to be taken by affected communities to prevent further arsen exposure for providing safe water for drinking, food preparation, and crop irrigation, include:  
- Replace high‑arsenic sources, like groundwater, with low‑arsenic sources, such as rainwater and treated surface water.  
- Use low‑arsenic water for drinking, cooking, and irrigation, and high‑arsenic water for bathing and washing clothes.  
- Identify high‑arsenic and low‑arsenic sources.  
- Test water and mark wells or pumps with different colors to indicate arsen levels.  
- Educate the community for effective use.  
- Mix low‑arsenic water with higher‑arsenic water to reach safe levels.  
- Install arsen removal systems (centralized or domestic) and ensure proper disposal of removed arsen.  
- Methods include oxidation, coagulation‑precipitation, absorption, ion exchange, and membrane techniques. There are more low‑cost and effective options available, though long‑term effectiveness needs further evidence.
